When I Say “New York Style”

Alright listen up, folks. If you wanna talk about New York’s good eats, you can’t leave out the kosher dill pickles. These crunchy babies are a deli and street cart staple, and have been satisfying hungry New Yorkers for generations.

Now, let me tell you something about the word “kosher” in my world. It doesn’t necessarily mean that these pickles follow Jewish dietary laws. Nope, “kosher” actually refers to the use of garlic in the pickling brine. And let me tell you, that garlic is what takes these pickles to the next level. It’s an old-school tradition that’s been around for ages, and it gives my pickles a flavor that’s truly one-of-a-kind.

My Pickles Are Fermented

Let me tell you something. I take my pickles seriously around here. Unlike those bland, supermarket pickles that are boiled and preserved with salt or vinegar, my kosher dill pickles are made the old-fashioned way, using a natural lacto-fermentation process (sv: mjölksyrning).

What’s lacto-fermentation, you ask? It’s a technique that’s been used for centuries, where the natural bacteria in the air and on the surface of the cucumbers are allowed to do their thing and create a delicious brine that results in a crisp, crunchy texture and a tangy, briny flavor that’ll knock your socks off.

And that’s not all. These pickles are packed with probiotics, meaning that they’re full of good bacteria. That’s real important for a healthy gut microbiome. So not only are these pickles delicious, but they’re good for you too.

Let Me Tell You About Cucumbers

Have a seat, because I’m about to drop some pickle knowledge on you. When it comes to pickling, you can use all kinds of cucumbers, but let me tell you, it’s worth it to get your hands on the freshest ones possible.

Now, in Sweden, you’re likely to come across English cucumbers in the supermarket. These long, thin-skinned cucumbers are available year-round and are wrapped in plastic to keep them fresh and reduce food waste. They have a mild flavor that works well for making pickle spears, so go ahead and slice them up.

But if it’s the summer, you can look for Västerås cucumbers. These small, firm cucumbers are grown outdoors, and they’re perfect for pickling. The only catch is that they’re only available from July to September.

So there you have it. The lowdown on cucumbers for pickling. Now get out there and start pickling!

That Brine Is Good Stuff

Don’t pour that pickle brine down the drain! You’d be wasting a precious resource. That brine is a goldmine of flavor that can give a boost to all kinds of dishes.

Don’t believe me? Try adding pickle brine to your salads, dressings, and marinades. It adds a tangy, briny kick that’ll take your dishes to the next level. Or, if you’re feeling adventurous, you can even make popsicles out of it. Yeah, you heard me right. Pickle brine popsicles. Trust me, they’re better than they sound.

And if you’re feeling thirsty, why not try using the brine in a cocktail or chaser? It adds a unique flavor that’ll make your drinks stand out from the crowd. (Just please drink responsibly, folks.)
