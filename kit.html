Let Me Tell You About Cucumbers

Alright I’m about to drop some cucumber knowledge on you. For pickling, you can use all types of cucumbers, but it’s sure worth it to get your hands on the freshest ones possible.

In Sweden, you’re most likely to come across English cucumbers (sv: slanggurkor). These long, thin-skinned cucumbers are available year-round in the supermarket and are wrapped in plastic to keep them fresh and reduce waste. They work really well for making pickle halves or spears (i.e. long wedges), so go ahead and slice them up.

Next time you’re in the supermarket, keep an eye out for mini cucumbers (sv: snackgurkor). They’re more expensive but they’re small and have thin skin, so they’re good for pickling.

If it’s the summer, you can try to get a hold of Västerås cucumbers (sv: västeråsgurkor). These small cucumbers are grown outdoors, so they have good flavor. The only catch is that they’re only available from July to September.

Here’s The Deal About Containers

Now, I know you’re probably picturing the traditional glass mason jar, but let me tell ya, there are more things to pickle in than you can shake a stick at.

First off, let’s talk about upcycling. You know, when you take something old and turn it into something new. You can pickle cucumbers in just about anything, from a plastic lunchbox container to an old bathtub.

The key to pickling success is making sure your cucumbers stay submerged in the brine. That’s why it’s important to use a container in which you can easily weigh down your pickles. It doesn’t have to be fancy or expensive, just something that’ll get the job done.

A simple trick is to place a small plate or saucer on top of your pickles to keep them submerged. If you want to get really crafty, you can even cut up a flexible plastic lid or container. Then, on top of your plate or lid, use a small jar or can as a weight to keep it all in place to prevent your pickles from reaching the surface.
