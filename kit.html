Let Me Tell You About Cucumbers

Alright I’m about to drop some cucumber knowledge on you. For pickling, you can use all types of cucumbers, but it’s sure worth it to get your hands on the freshest ones possible.

In Sweden, you’re most likely to come across English cucumbers (sv: slanggurkor). These long, thin-skinned cucumbers are available year-round in the supermarket and are wrapped in plastic to keep them fresh and reduce waste. They work really well for making pickle halves or spears (i.e. long wedges). Just slice them up.

That said, you’ll want to keep an eye out for mini cucumbers (sv: snackgurkor). They’re more expensive but worth the extra. They’re firm and thin-skinned and perfectly pickle-sized. In other words, they’re ideal for pickling!

If it’s the summer, you can try to get a hold of Västerås cucumbers (sv: västeråsgurkor). These small-ish cucumbers are grown outdoors, so they have good flavor. The only catch is that they’re only available from July to September.

You Don’t Need a Fancy Container

Now, I know you’re probably picturing that pretty traditional glass mason jar, but let me tell ya, there are more things to pickle in than you can shake a stick at.

First off, let’s talk about upcycling. You know, when you take something old and turn it into something new. You can pickle cucumbers in just about anything, from last week’s ice cream container to an old bathtub.

The key to pickling success is making sure your cucumbers stay submerged in the brine. That’s why it’s important to use a container in which you can easily weigh down your pickles. It doesn’t have to be fancy or expensive, just something that’ll get the job done.

A simple trick is to place a small plate or saucer on top of your pickles to keep them submerged. If you want to get really crafty, you can even up a flexible plastic lid or container. Then, on top of that plate or lid, use a small jar or can as a weight to keep it all in place and prevent your pickles from floating to the surface.

The Deal About Salt

If you’re checking out the ingredients of Pickle John’s Kosher Dill D.I.Y. Pickling Kit, you might be curious about that calcium chloride (sv: kalciumklorid). Well, there’s not need to worry. Calcium chloride is a specialty salt, used in making many common food products — like in cheese, beer, and tofu. It can be naturally found in seawater, and it’s considered safe by food safety regulatory agencies.

Of course, we also use sea salt, a.k.a. sodium chloride (sv: natriumklorid), to enhance flavor and draw out excess water. That’s important for achieving that signature pickle crunch. But we don’t want to use too much regular salt. It’s not healthy to consume a ton of sodium, folks!

That’s where the calcium chloride comes in. It strengthens the natural pectin in veggies, helping to maintain the texture of the cucumbers and prevent them from becoming mushy. The result: crispy pickles that stay crispy — all while using less regular salt.
